About
the Work

Environmental Anthropology Indigenous Studies Political Economy Medical History

I am a sociocultural and environmental anthropologist who studies the politics of land, health, and resource extraction in northern British Columbia — working alongside Dakelh First Nations communities to understand how Indigenous governance operates in the aftermath of industrial forestry.

My doctoral research focuses on the Nak'azdli Whut'en and Binche Whut'en peoples. Through sixteen months of ethnographic fieldwork with logging companies, hunters, elders, and forestry officials — complemented by archival, oral-historical, and GIS analyses — I trace how forestry transformed Dakelh forests into "log lands."

Working in genuine partnership with these communities, I co-designed the research questions, established oversight committees, and return annually to maintain accountability and reciprocity. Since 2023 I have also helped coordinate the Binche Dakelh science summer camp.

My broader scholarly interests span environmental anthropology, Indigenous sovereignty, colonial law, public health, race, and eugenics. Earlier I trained as a biomedical researcher, studying immunology and cancer.

02

Research

Current and ongoing projects at the intersection of Indigenous resurgence, settler-colonial governance, and resource capitalism in the Canadian North.

Project 01

The Second Growth

Book project examining Indigenous resurgence, settler adaptation, and the possibility of repair in the log lands of the Canadian North. Traces how forestry transformed Dakelh forests and how First Nations leaders negotiate authority over these landscapes.

Project 02

Legitimacy Without Liberalism

Dissertation chapter examining how the Nak'azdli Whut'en govern mixed Indigenous-settler polities through infrastructure investment — splash parks, public goods, community services — outside liberal consent frameworks.

Project 03

Reconciliation Capitalism

Theoretical framework analyzing how "reconciled logging practices" function as a mode of Indigenous resurgence — reclaiming territorial authority through, rather than simply against, resource capitalism. Winner of Harold K. Schneider Prize.

Project 04

Colonial Public Health

Historical research on race, eugenics, and forced sterilization in colonial public health contexts, integrated into a broader critique of biopolitics and Indigenous health sovereignty.
