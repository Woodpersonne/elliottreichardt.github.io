Studying the politics of land and resource extraction in northern British Columbia — working alongside Dakelh First Nations and rural communities.

My doctoral research focuses on the Nak'azdli Whut'en and Binche Whut'en peoples. Through sixteen months of ethnographic fieldwork with logging companies, hunters, elders, and forestry officials — complemented by archival, oral-historical, and GIS analyses — I trace how forestry transformed Dakelh forests into "log lands."

Working collaboratively with these communities, I co-designed the research questions, established oversight committees, and return annually to maintain accountability and reciprocity. Since 2023 I have helped coordinate the Binche Dakelh science summer camp.

My broader scholarly interests span environmental anthropology, Indigenous sovereignty, colonial law, public health, race, and eugenics. Earlier I trained as a biomedical researcher at the University of Calgary, studying immunology and cancer. In 2026 I will join DePauw University as Assistant Professor of Anthropology.

Research Projects

Book Project

The Second Growth

Examining Indigenous resurgence, settler adaptation, and the possibility of repair in the log lands of the Canadian North. Dissertation: The Second Growth: Indigenous Resurgence, Settler Adaptation, and the Possibility of Repair in the Log Lands of the Canadian North.

Chapter

Legitimacy Without Liberalism

How the Nak'azdli Whut'en govern mixed Indigenous-settler polities through infrastructure investment — splash parks, public goods, community services — outside liberal consent frameworks.

Article

Reconciliation Capitalism

Theoretical framework analyzing how "reconciled logging practices" function as a mode of Indigenous resurgence — reclaiming territorial authority through, rather than simply against, resource capitalism.

Harold K. Schneider Prize 2025

Historical

Colonial Public Health & Eugenics

Historical research on race, eugenics, and forced sterilization in colonial public health contexts — integrating a broader critique of biopolitics and Indigenous health sovereignty.
